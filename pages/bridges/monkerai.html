About Monkerai Bridge

Monkerai Bridge over the Karuah River is a 70‘ (21 m) Bennett Truss (also called Old PWD – Public Works Department – truss) located on a little used unsealed road in a remote rural setting about 22 km northeast of Dungog. It was originally completed in 1882. The bridge had extensive modifications made to it over the years which damaged both its structural performance and heritage integrity. It was in a state of disrepair and closed to traffic from 2004 to 2020. Monkerai Bridge was fully upgraded in 2019, taking approximately sixteen months to completion and reopening to traffic in May 2020.

Monkerai Bridge is of State significance as the second oldest surviving timber truss road bridge in New South Wales. It is rare and representative as the only remaining example of a 70’ Bennett truss and as one of only two remaining examples of Bennett trusses of approximately 150 built. William Christopher Bennett was the designer of this truss type. Bennett was recognised as one of the ablest officers in the government service, and held the position of Commissioner and Chief Engineer to the Roads Department of the New South Wales Department of Public Works from 1862 until 1889.

Bennett trusses are historically significant as the first of the five stages of timber truss road bridge design in New South Wales, which proved and popularised the timber truss as the preferred form of bridge construction for medium span bridges in New South Wales. It is historically significant through its association with the expansion of the State road network, and the contribution of that road system to settlement, development and economic activity. At the time the Bennett trusses were being built hardwoods were plentiful in NSW. The design is an example of innovative and practical engineering in a time when large and long old growth timbers were readily available and vast numbers of bridges were being built on a tight budget.

The bridge is approximately 100 m long and 5.5 m wide, consisting of three timber girder approach spans of varying lengths (approximately 8.5 m, 12.2 m and 12.3 m respectively) and three 70’ (21.336 m) Bennett type truss spans. The six spans are supported on five timber trestle piers and two timber abutments. It was designed to carry a single lane of traffic.


The diagram shows the general arrangement of Monkerai Bridge

Monkerai Bridge is one example of the Bennett design which has performed well beyond the expectations of the original designer, with the average life of the Bennett Truss being 54 years, 26 bridges remaining in service beyond 80 years, and only two still remaining today after 135 years.

Timeline

Pre 1800s

For thousands of years prior to the arrival of Europeans the Karuah Valley was, and still is, inhabited by the Worimi, who lived a vibrant healthy lifestyle afforded by foods of the valley like kangaroo, fish and yabbies from the local waters. The Worimi are members of the Gathang (gu-thung) language group, the language spoken from as far south as Port Stephens to Port Macquarie in the north.

Recent artefacts identified at the Monkerai Bridge site indicate Worimi activity in the immediate area. The artefacts were uncovered on the nearby flood plains and included several hammer stones and cores used for making sharp flakes for tools and spear points. The hammer stones appear to be cobbles sourced from the Karuah River.

1820s

W.C Bennett (bridge designer) is born in Ireland on 4th July 1824. In the same year, the Australian Agricultural Company is grated 1 million acres from Port Stephens to the Manning River for agricultural farming. The estate included the Karuah River and Gloucester River Valleys. Robert Dawson, Chief Agent for the Australian Agricultural Company, arrived with the first group of 79 settlers to explore the country around Maitland and Newcastle.

1850s

Cedar cutters move into the area seeking the valuable timber. Logs felled from the surrounding valley are transported by bullock wagon and steam train to be shipped to Port Stephens. In 1855 the first timber truss bridge was constructed in NSW at Carcoar, and Bennett arrived in NSW the same year, having worked in Ireland, Central America, England and New Zealand.

1870s

The Weismantels-Dingadee Road (now Monkerai Road) through the Monkerai Valley is the main road link between the villages of Dungog, Gloucester and Stroud, and is the best route to the Barrington goldfields. However, the road is often impassable for days in times of heavy rain and flood. Gold is discovered in 1875 by timber collectors in the Barrington Tops region which sees miners flock to the area. In 1878 gravel begin being laid down to improve the roads and a new Monkerai Hill road is built.

1882

Monkerai Bridge is opened.

1889

W.C. Bennett dies on 29th September 1889, having overseen the construction of nearly 16,000 km of roads in NSW and nearly 65 km of bridges (including approximately 150 Bennett type timber truss bridges).

1890

Most of the gold mining activity had ceased by this time and overcutting of cedar leads to a depletion of supplies.

2004

The bridge closes to traffic due to safety concerns from deterioration.

2020

The bridge is reopened after a $15M refurbishment, bringing Bennett’s original design back into service with strength and safety improvements.
